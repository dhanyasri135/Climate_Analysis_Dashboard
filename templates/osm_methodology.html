📊 Methodology & Calculations

Understanding the OSM Gap Analysis Framework

← Back to OSM Gap Analysis

Overview

This gap analysis evaluates OpenStreetMap (OSM) coverage of climate resilience infrastructure across 43 global megacities. The methodology combines quantitative scoring with qualitative assessment to identify critical data gaps that limit researchers' ability to model urban climate adaptation.

1. OSM Coverage Score Calculation

The OSM Coverage Score represents the overall completeness of climate-relevant infrastructure data in OpenStreetMap for each city, ranging from 0-100%.

Calculation Method

Formula:
OSM Coverage Score = Weighted Average of 7 Feature Categories
Coverage = Σ (Feature_Score × Weight) / Σ Weights

Feature Categories & Weights

Feature Category Weight Reasoning
Drainage Systems Mapped 20% Critical for flood modeling
Green Infrastructure 15% Essential for heat mitigation
Cooling Centers 15% Vital for heat adaptation
Evacuation Routes 15% Critical for emergency planning
Emergency Shelters 15% Essential for disaster response
Flood Barriers 10% Important for flood protection
Permeable Surfaces 10% Important for stormwater management

Coverage Level Scoring

Each feature category is assessed on a three-level scale:

  • High (100 points): Comprehensive mapping with detailed attributes (>70% of infrastructure mapped)
  • Medium (50 points): Partial mapping with basic features (30-70% mapped)
  • Low (25 points): Minimal or incomplete mapping (<30% mapped)
Example Calculation: Tokyo
  • Drainage: Medium (50) × 0.20 = 10.0
  • Green Infrastructure: High (100) × 0.15 = 15.0
  • Cooling Centers: High (100) × 0.15 = 15.0
  • Evacuation Routes: High (100) × 0.15 = 15.0
  • Emergency Shelters: High (100) × 0.15 = 15.0
  • Flood Barriers: Medium (50) × 0.10 = 5.0
  • Permeable Surfaces: Medium (50) × 0.10 = 5.0
Total OSM Coverage Score = 80.0%

2. Gap Severity Score

The Gap Severity Score quantifies how significant the data gaps are for each city, calculated as the inverse of coverage.

Formula:
Gap Severity = 100 - OSM Coverage Score
Higher scores indicate more severe data gaps

Gap Severity Classification

Gap Severity Range Category Interpretation
≥70% Critical Severe data gaps limiting all resilience modeling
50-69% Severe Major gaps preventing comprehensive analysis
30-49% Moderate Significant gaps in specific feature categories
<30% Minor Good coverage with opportunities for enhancement

3. Priority Level Assignment

Each city is assigned a Mapping Priority Level (High/Medium/Low) based on the combination of gap severity and climate vulnerability.

Priority Determination Criteria

Priority Criteria Rationale
HIGH OSM Coverage < 60%
AND
High climate risk (flood/heat/drought) 		Cities with both severe data gaps and high climate vulnerability require immediate mapping efforts
MEDIUM OSM Coverage 60-75%
OR
Moderate climate risk 		Cities with moderate gaps or moderate risks benefit from targeted mapping campaigns
LOW OSM Coverage ≥ 75%
AND
Good baseline data exists 		Cities with strong existing coverage can focus on data refinement and updates

4. Critical Gaps Identification

For each city, we identify the top 3 critical gaps - specific infrastructure features that are most urgently needed for climate resilience modeling.

Identification Process

  1. Feature Assessment: Evaluate all 7 feature categories for completeness
  2. Climate Risk Correlation: Match missing features to city-specific climate risks
    • Coastal cities → Prioritize flood barriers, sea level rise infrastructure
    • Arid climates → Prioritize cooling centers, water infrastructure
    • High flood risk → Prioritize drainage systems, evacuation routes
  3. Impact Ranking: Rank gaps by potential impact on resilience modeling accuracy
  4. Top 3 Selection: Select the three most critical gaps per city
Example: Mumbai (Coastal, High Flood Risk)
Critical Gaps Identified:
  1. Coastal flood barriers - Essential for sea level rise modeling
  2. Drainage systems - Critical for monsoon flood prediction
  3. Shelter locations - Vital for evacuation planning

5. Regional Coverage Analysis

Regional statistics aggregate coverage data by world region to identify geographic patterns.

Calculation:
Regional Avg Coverage = Σ (City Coverage Scores) / Number of Cities in Region

Regional Classifications

  • North America: USA, Mexico, Canada
  • South America: Brazil, Argentina, Peru, Colombia
  • Europe: UK, France, Turkey, Russia
  • East Asia: Japan, China, South Korea
  • South Asia: India, Pakistan, Bangladesh
  • Southeast Asia: Indonesia, Philippines, Thailand, Vietnam
  • Africa: Egypt, Nigeria, DR Congo
  • Middle East: Iran, Gulf states

6. Feature Coverage Summary

Global statistics summarize how many cities have High/Medium/Low coverage for each feature category.

Calculation for Each Feature:
High Coverage Count = Number of cities with "High" rating
Medium Coverage Count = Number of cities with "Medium" rating
Low Coverage Count = Number of cities with "Low" rating
Coverage Percentage = (High Count / Total Cities) × 100

7. Data Sources & Assessment Method

Primary Data Sources

  • OpenStreetMap Database (2025-2026): Direct analysis of OSM data completeness via Overpass API queries for each of the 43 megacities. Extracted infrastructure features including drainage systems, green spaces, public facilities, and emergency infrastructure.
  • OSM Taginfo: Global feature usage statistics and tagging pattern analysis to assess completeness of climate-relevant attributes (capacity, type, accessibility, etc.).
  • HOT Tasking Manager: Humanitarian OpenStreetMap Team mapping campaign coverage data to identify areas with recent organized mapping efforts.
  • Research Literature: Academic studies on OSM data quality including:
    • Haklay et al. (2010) - "How Good is Volunteered Geographical Information?"
    • Barrington-Leigh & Millard-Ball (2017) - "Global Road Completeness"
    • Senaratne et al. (2017) - "A Review of Volunteered Geographic Information Quality"
  • Government Open Data Portals: Official infrastructure inventories from municipal and national governments used as reference datasets for validation:
    • NYC Open Data (United States)
    • data.gov.uk (United Kingdom)
    • Tokyo Metropolitan Government Open Data (Japan)
    • Paris Open Data (France)
  • UN-Habitat Urban Resilience Database: Climate vulnerability and adaptation indicators for megacities.
  • C40 Cities Climate Leadership Group: Infrastructure and resilience data from member cities.
  • World Bank Climate Change Knowledge Portal: Climate risk profiles and adaptation metrics.
  • IPCC Regional Assessment Reports: Climate impact projections and infrastructure vulnerability data.

Assessment Methodology

  1. Automated Analysis (January-February 2026): OSM data extraction via Overpass API for each city using standardized bounding boxes. Queries targeted specific infrastructure tags:
    • Drainage: waterway=drain, man_made=wastewater_plant, tunnel=culvert
    • Green infrastructure: landuse=grass|forest, leisure=park, natural=wood
    • Cooling centers: amenity=cooling_center, building=public_hall (with cooling tags)
    • Emergency shelters: amenity=shelter, emergency=assembly_point
    • Flood barriers: barrier=flood_gate, man_made=dyke|levee
  2. Feature Counting: Quantify mapped features in each category per city, normalized by city area to ensure comparability across different city sizes.
  3. Attribute Completeness Analysis: Assess presence of critical attributes (capacity, type, material, height, etc.) beyond basic geometry. Completeness scored as percentage of features with ≥3 detailed attributes.
  4. Expert Review: Manual validation of automated assessments by reviewing sample areas in each city using OSM editors (iD, JOSM) and satellite imagery comparison.
  5. Comparison with Reference Data: Cross-validate against known infrastructure inventories from government sources where available. For 18 cities with published infrastructure data, OSM coverage validated against official counts.
  6. Researcher Consultation (December 2025): Interviews with 12 climate adaptation researchers to identify priority features and data requirements for resilience modeling.
Data Collection Period: Primary OSM data analysis conducted December 2025 - February 2026. Climate and vulnerability data reflects most recent available datasets (2023-2025). OSM is continuously updated; coverage scores represent point-in-time assessment.

8. Limitations & Considerations

Important Limitations:
  • Assessment Timing: OSM data is constantly evolving; scores reflect point-in-time analysis
  • Tagging Variability: Different mapping conventions across regions may affect comparability
  • Access Restrictions: Some infrastructure data may be intentionally restricted for security
  • Data Quality: Presence of features doesn't guarantee attribute completeness or accuracy
  • Weighting Subjectivity: Feature weights reflect research priorities but may vary by use case

9. Statistical Analysis Methods

Summary Statistics

  • Mean Coverage: Average OSM coverage across all 43 cities
  • Standard Deviation: Variability in coverage scores
  • Percentiles: Distribution of coverage (25th, 50th, 75th percentiles)
  • Range: Minimum and maximum coverage scores

Visualizations

  • Bar Charts: Show gap severity rankings (Plotly library)
  • Heatmaps: Display feature coverage across cities (color-coded)
  • Pie Charts: Illustrate priority level distribution
  • Scatter Plots: Correlate coverage with gap severity
  • Stacked Bars: Compare High/Medium/Low coverage globally
